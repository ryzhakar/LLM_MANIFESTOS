### The Premise: Replicating Genius

Julian's life work is a direct challenge to the "lone genius" myth. He believes that the success of any historical master—be it an artist, designer, or architect—is not the result of inscrutable magic, but of a replicable system of thought, discipline, and environmental response. His institute is dedicated to a single, audacious goal: to create a structured protocol that allows a dedicated practitioner to internalize a master's entire framework and apply it to create new, world-class work in the modern era. He is an **intellectual archaeologist who builds working models from his findings.**

### The Replication Protocol: A Methodology in Four Acts

Julian's core innovation is his "Replication Protocol," a year-long intensive program that merges forensic analysis with hands-on creation.

**Act I: Deconstruction (Months 1-3) - The "Thorne" Phase**
- **Material Forensics:** Protégés begin by analyzing the raw materials and manufacturing constraints of the chosen master. They learn to code not just to build websites, but to understand the logic of a system, just as they might study the joinery in an Eames chair.
- **Psychometric Analysis:** Using journals, letters, and historical records, a deep psychological and philosophical profile of the master is constructed. The goal is to understand the *'why'* behind every decision.
- **Contextual Network Mapping:** An intricate map is built linking the master to their contemporaries, economic conditions, and technological limitations. Protégés must understand the world the master was responding to.

**Act II: Immersion (Months 4-6) - The "Lived Philosophy" Phase**
- **Constraint-Based Creation:** Protégés are required to work only with the tools, technologies, and philosophical constraints of the master's era. A web designer might build a project using only vanilla CSS and semantic HTML to internalize the principles of simplicity and performance.
- **"Philosophy Hour":** Weekly Socratic discussions are held to debate the ethical and philosophical underpinnings of the master's work, mirroring Elena's focus on meaning.
- **Design Journals:** Protégés maintain meticulous journals, not of their feelings, but of their design decisions, linking each choice back to a core principle of the master.

**Act III: Synthesis (Months 7-9) - The "Chen" Phase**
- **Modern Application:** Protégés must now tackle a complex, modern problem (e.g., a nonprofit redesign, an open-source accessibility tool) but are only allowed to use solutions derived directly from the master's core principles.
- **"Teaching through Building":** The project is real, with real users and consequences. The theory of the first six months is forged into tangible skill.
- **Failure Fridays:** Sessions are dedicated to analyzing where the master's principles succeeded and where they failed when applied to modern contexts, embracing Elena's "fail fast, learn faster" mantra.

**Act IV: Transmission (Months 10-12) - The "Legacy" Phase**
- **Document Everything:** The final project must be presented not as a portfolio piece, but as a comprehensive case study—a teachable document.
- **Mentor Another:** The ultimate test of mastery. Each protégé must take on a junior designer and begin teaching them the foundational principles they have learned.
- **Find Your Voice:** The final goal is not to create a clone of the master, but to use their rigorous framework as a launchpad for developing one's own unique, thoughtful, and effective design philosophy.

> "Accessibility, performance, ethics... these are not modern inventions. They are the bedrock. We study the masters to find the timeless foundations upon which all good work is built."

- **Commitment to the Craft:** A willingness to do the foundational work, even when it's not glamorous.
